Feral Swine Population Research: Challenges in Data Collection and Reporting

by: Scott Cormier

Abstract

This study is a brief examination of the methodological challenges and reporting discrepancies in freely-available data about feral swine (Sus scrofa) population in the United States. Through systematic review of publicly available data sources, including state wildlife agencies, agricultural departments, and specialized hunting resources, I identified significant inconsistencies in estimation methodologies, reporting standards, and data accessibility. Findings reveal wide variations in population reporting formats, from single-point estimates to broad numerical ranges, complicating cross-state comparisons. I conclude that developing standardized population estimation protocols, improving coordination between state agencies, and establishing transparent data sharing mechanisms are essential for improving feral swine population monitoring and management effectiveness.

Introduction

Feral swine (Sus scrofa (feral type) Linnaeus) represent a significant ecological and agricultural challenge in the United States, with an estimated population exceeding 6 million. Recognized as one of the world's most destructive invasive species, these animals pose substantial threats to agricultural ecosystems and rural economies [1, 2].

While the biological and ecological dimensions of the feral swine invasion have received substantial scholarly attention, considerably less research has focused on the methodological challenges inherent in population monitoring and the resulting data inconsistencies that complicate management efforts. Studies have attempted to quantify feral swine populations at state or regional levels using various methodologies, including camera trapping (Massei et al., 2018), mark-recapture techniques (Hanson et al., 2008), and aerial surveys (Campbell et al., 2010). However, these approaches vary significantly in their implementation, accuracy, and geographic coverage. The resulting heterogeneity in population estimation methodologies creates substantial challenges for policymakers and wildlife managers attempting to allocate resources effectively or evaluate management success across jurisdictional boundaries. [2b, 2c, 2d]

I have limited access to studies that can be found behind paywalls, but it seems clear that there are no standards to determine how population data for feral swine should be collected, reported, and utilized across different states and agencies. This knowledge gap impedes the development of coordinated management strategies and prevents accurate assessment of population trends at regional or national scales. It also prevents researchers from effectively combining their data and perhaps reaching useful conclusions.

The objective of this article is to systematically examine the methodological approaches, reporting practices, and data inconsistencies in feral swine population estimation across the United States. My focus is on research information that is readily available to members of the public who search for information on the topic online.

  1. Document the varying methodologies employed by state agencies and research organizations for estimating feral swine populations.
  2. Analyze discrepancies in reporting formats and practices that complicate cross-state comparisons.
  3. Identify promising approaches for standardizing population monitoring and reporting.
  4. Provide recommendations for improving data collection, sharing, and utilization in feral swine management.

By addressing these objectives, this study seeks to contribute to the development of more effective, coordinated approaches to monitoring and managing one of North America's most consequential invasive species challenges.

Data Collection Challenges

As wild animals, feral swine are resistant to being tracked and counted, and their behaviors change when they are hunted. Falyn Owens, a wildlife biologist with the North Carolina Wildlife Resources Commission, explained that "We don't in North Carolina have a good estimate of the population of feral swine across the state because it's a really, really hard number to pin down"[3].

Preferred Approach: Report Sightings

What seems to be the best of the current approaches, for research purposes, relies heavily on reported observations and consistent documentation. The University of Georgia's Center for Invasive Species and Ecosystem Health's Early Detection & Distribution Mapping System (EDDMapS) offers records dated July 1, 1982, to October 30, 2024, and include validated data entered by informed observers and made freely available to everyone on their website.[4].

Methodological Reflection

Lack of standardization is causing researchers to miss opportunities, and data gathered about feral hogs is not always useful. For example, Texas has a high number of sightings, and a large population. The data does not adjust for the fact that Texas is the largest state.

  1. The Arkansas Department of Agriculture records "removal" data on the number of feral hogs that are killed, but other states refer to killing hogs with different labels, such as "harvesting," or do not track deaths at all.[5]
  2. How population data is formatted is also important, especially if we are comparing results from one state to another:

    Texas reports "about 2.6 million" feral swine,[6] Georgia estimates "200,000 to 600,000",[7] Florida indicates "over one-half million."[8] If some report the high data, some the low, others what is observed, and still others the estimated number or a range for the population data, then it is much harder for researchers to compile existing data and come to conclusions.

  3. Some of the most interesting materials that I found online were charts and graphs from hunting sites. While such sources may provide valuable insights, they often lack rigorous scientific documentation and verifiability. Comparative analysis of multiple sources, including specialized hunting websites and state-level reports, reveals significant differences, while also showing the challenges and potential in feral swine population tracking.

The proliferation of hunting-focused websites has contributed significantly to public knowledge about feral swine populations. While sites such as those maintained by recreational hunting organizations [9, 10] provide accessible and well-organized data presentations, their utility for academic research is limited by inconsistent source attribution. Conclusion

The study of feral swine populations is important, and requires continued methodological refinement to allow the development of coordinated, evidence-based strategies. The biological and behavioral characteristics of feral swine—their adaptability, elusive nature, and behavioral responses to hunting pressure—create inherent difficulties in population estimation that transcend conventional wildlife monitoring approaches.

The divergent metrics employed by different states—from precise numerical estimates to broad ranges, from active monitoring to passive reporting—create a fragmented data landscape that precludes meaningful aggregation and comparison. This inconsistency extends to basic terminology, with states variously tracking "removal," "harvest," or "take" data with different methodological approaches. Raw population figures, when not adjusted for state size, habitat availability, or agricultural distribution, provide an incomplete picture of invasion intensity and associated ecological and economic impacts. A more standardized approach is needed: one that takes advantage of non-traditional data sources, particularly those from recreational hunting platforms.

The University of Georgia's Early Detection & Distribution Mapping System (EDDMapS) is the most promising approach to feral swine monitoring that I have found so far, in that it leverages validated observational data from diverse sources. If more universities could provide ways for interdisciplinary collaborators like farmers and hunters to supply usable data, and cooperate to standardize reporting, then research experts could then compile and study the results to reach conclusions, and everyone would benefit.

The feral swine invasion represents a complex ecological, economic, and methodological challenge that requires coordinated response across multiple domains. While perfect population estimation may remain elusive, significant improvements in data quality, consistency, and utility are achievable through collaborative approaches that span disciplinary and geographical boundaries.
